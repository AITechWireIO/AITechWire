Scaramucci: Front-running scheme on Truth Social, long gold/BTC

Anthony Scaramucci highlighted a potential millisecond advantage scheme on Trump tweets for hedge funds and warned of inflation from $55T deficits. He reiterated bullish stance on gold and Bitcoin.

AI TechWire · July 22, 2026 · 5 min read
Photo by Giorgio Trovato / Unsplash

Anthony Scaramucci has raised pointed concerns about what he describes as a potential millisecond-advantage scheme operating around posts on Truth Social. The allegation: certain hedge funds may be positioned to act on presidential communications — specifically market-moving posts — faster than the rest of the market can react.

This isn't a fringe claim. It's a structural concern about information asymmetry at the highest possible level. If true, it represents a fundamental corruption of price discovery — the kind of edge that would make conventional front-running look quaint. Retail investors, pension funds, and sophisticated institutions alike would be operating in a tilted market, reacting to news that others have already traded against.

The implications for regulators are severe. The SEC has historically struggled to police social media-driven volatility. A coordinated millisecond advantage tied to a sitting president's feed would represent an entirely new enforcement challenge — one that existing frameworks are arguably not built to handle.

"If hedge funds are front-running presidential social media posts, the rational response isn't to trust the system — it's to own assets the system cannot debase."

- AITechWire Analysis

The Fiscal Backdrop: A Structural Case for Hard Assets

Beyond the trading integrity question, Scaramucci's deeper macro argument centers on fiscal reality. His warnings about ballooning U.S. deficits aren't abstract doom-mongering — they're an assessment of structural inflation risk baked into the trajectory of federal spending.

When a sovereign continuously spends beyond its means, the historical resolution is almost always the same: inflate the debt away. Bondholders get repaid in depreciated dollars. Savers get punished. And assets with finite supply — gold, Bitcoin — become the rational hedge.

This is precisely the environment in which Scaramucci's bullish stance on both gold and Bitcoin becomes not just defensible but arguably logical. Real yields matter. And in an environment of sustained fiscal expansion, the case for hard assets strengthens regardless of near-term price action.

Bitcoin and Gold: The Hard Asset Case

Scaramucci's reiterated bullish stance on both gold and Bitcoin arrives at a moment when the macro setup for hard assets is compelling. Gold's millennia-long track record as a store of value becomes a feature, not a nostalgic throwback, in an environment of weakening confidence in institutional guardrails — including, now, the integrity of market information flows.

Bitcoin plays a complementary role: a fixed-supply asset in a world of expanding sovereign liabilities. In this framing, both gold and Bitcoin aren't just inflation hedges — they're institutional trust hedges.

The Bottom Line

Scaramucci's thesis is coherent and uncomfortable: the institutions that are supposed to protect market participants — regulatory oversight, fiscal discipline, information fairness — are being tested simultaneously. Hard assets aren't a trade in this environment. They're a verdict on the current moment.
