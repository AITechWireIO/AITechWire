Scaramucci Sounds the Alarm: Front-Running on Truth Social, a $55T Debt Hole, and Why You Should Be Stacking Gold and Bitcoin

If you want a blunt read on where macro risk is headed, Anthony Scaramucci is rarely short of opinions — and right now, he's warning that the combination of alleged millisecond information advantages on presidential social media posts and a looming $55 trillion deficit spiral is building a powerful, if uncomfortable, case for hard assets.

Against a backdrop of Bitcoin reclaiming ground — trading around $65,968 as of July 22, 2026 — and gold reasserting its safe-haven credentials, Scaramucci's warnings land with particular force. The question isn't whether fiscal dysfunction is real. The question is whether investors are positioned for it.

The Truth Social Edge: Milliseconds, Hedge Funds, and Market Integrity

Scaramucci has raised pointed concerns about what he describes as a potential millisecond-advantage scheme operating around posts on Truth Social. The allegation is straightforward and damning in its implications: certain hedge funds may be positioned to act on presidential communications — specifically market-moving tweets — faster than the rest of the market can blink.

This isn't a fringe conspiracy. It's a structural concern about information asymmetry at the highest possible level. If true, it represents a fundamental corruption of price discovery — the kind of edge that would make conventional front-running look quaint by comparison. Retail investors, pension funds, and even sophisticated institutional players would be operating in a rigged casino, reacting to news that others have already traded against.

The implications for market regulators are severe. The SEC has historically struggled to police social media-driven volatility. A coordinated millisecond advantage tied to a sitting president's feed would represent an entirely new category of enforcement challenge — one that existing frameworks are arguably not built to handle.

$55 Trillion in Deficits: Inflation Is the Feature, Not the Bug

Beyond the trading integrity question, Scaramucci's deeper macro argument centers on fiscal reality. His warning about $55 trillion in projected deficits isn't abstract doom-mongering — it's an assessment of structural inflation risk baked into the U.S. fiscal trajectory.

The arithmetic is brutal. When a sovereign continuously spends beyond its means at that scale, the historical resolution is almost always the same: inflate the debt away. Bondholders get repaid in depreciated dollars. Savers get punished. And assets with finite supply — gold, Bitcoin — become the rational hedge.

This is precisely the environment in which Scaramucci's bullish stance on both gold and Bitcoin becomes not just defensible but arguably obvious. Real yields matter. As CoinDesk's analysis of Bitcoin pricing notes, both BTC and Nasdaq valuations adjusted for the cost of capital represented by the U.S. 10-year yield have failed to eclipse their 2020-2021 peaks — even as dollar-denominated prices set new record highs. In other words, the bull run in hard assets, when measured properly against the cost of capital, may have significant room to run.

Bitcoin and Gold: The Hard Asset Case Gets Harder

Scaramucci's reiterated bullish stance on both gold and Bitcoin arrives at a moment when the macro setup for hard assets is arguably the most compelling it has been in years. Bitcoin, trading near $65,968 on July 22, 2026, is regaining its footing after a pullback from last year's $126,000 peak, with market participants debating whether the current recovery is the start of a decisive new leg higher.

The CoinDesk Daybook, published July 22, 2026, flags a critical nuance: optimism has returned, and several observers are calling the recent price rise the beginning of a bull run targeting valuations well beyond the $126,000 peak. But the cost-of-capital-adjusted view urges caution about the pace of that move. The BTC/US10Y ratio tells a more measured story than raw dollar prices suggest.

That nuance, paradoxically, is precisely what makes the long-term structural case stronger rather than weaker. If Bitcoin hasn't yet eclipsed its 2020-2021 highs on a real, yield-adjusted basis, and if the fiscal backdrop is deteriorating at the pace Scaramucci describes, the runway for appreciation in hard assets is longer than most bulls are even claiming.

Gold plays a complementary role. In an environment of sustained fiscal expansion, currency debasement, and weakening confidence in institutional guardrails — including, now, the integrity of market information flows — gold's millennia-long track record as a store of value becomes a feature, not a nostalgic throwback.

The Conclusion Writes Itself — And It's Not Comfortable

Scaramucci's thesis is, at its core, a coherent and uncomfortable one: the institutions that are supposed to protect market participants — regulatory oversight, fiscal discipline, information fairness — are failing simultaneously. If hedge funds are front-running presidential social media posts, and if the federal government is on a trajectory to accumulate $55 trillion in deficits, then the rational response isn't to trust the system. It's to own assets the system cannot debase.

Gold and Bitcoin aren't just inflation hedges in this framing. They're institutional trust hedges. And given the signals currently emanating from Washington and Wall Street alike, that case has never been more sharply articulated — or more urgently relevant.

Investors who dismiss Scaramucci's warnings as punditry do so at their own peril. The numbers, the structure, and the incentives all point in the same direction. Hard assets aren't a trade. They're a verdict on the current moment.
